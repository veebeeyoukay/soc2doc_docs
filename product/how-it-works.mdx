The whole process, including the parts most tools gloss over: who sees your
report, what the review contains, what it deliberately leaves out, and what
happens after. NDA first, then upload — in that order, every time.

## The three steps

<Steps>
  <Step title="NDA and upload">
    You sign a mutual NDA and give explicit permission for analysis by our systems
    and our experts. Then — and only then — the upload is enabled. Nothing leaves
    your hands before the NDA is executed. The NDA text is public at
    [soc2doc.ai/nda](https://soc2doc.ai/nda).
  </Step>
  <Step title="Extraction, then verification">
    AI pulls out every commitment, frequency, third-party dependency, and inherited
    control. Then a practitioner who has built SOC 2 programs checks the work before
    it ships. The machine reads; a human verifies.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Your review">
    Your Commitment Review arrives by email: everything you promised, how often, and
    what demonstrating it will take. No framework jargon, no control numbers. The
    same day.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## What the review is — and is not

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="In: every commitment and cadence" icon="check">
    What you promised and how often, organized by business function, with a
    "who owns this today?" prompt for each item.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Out: scoring and checklists" icon="x">
    The review does not do gap analysis and does not attach DIY templates. It mirrors
    the report; it does not grade the organization. See
    [the Commitment Review](/product/commitment-review) for why.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## Then what

Most teams find 30–60 commitments they cannot name an owner for. When that happens,
the path is a free urgent compliance call, then — if you want the help — year-round
management on the One Plan. Or your team takes the list and runs it alone. The
review is yours either way. See [after the review](/product/after-the-review).
